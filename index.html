Approach

This section describes the design decisions, including trajectory and MDP design.

Trajectory

A trajectory is defined as a sequence of $(\textbf{r}, t)$ points. In this implementation, trajectories are always periodic with the starting point having a position vector equal to the end point.

On initialisation, the robot uses a cubic spline from scipy to interpolate between the provided waypoints. This interpolation produces a target position for every RL policy timestep.

Further, the analytic derivative of this spline is also calculated and used as a target velocity. The target velocity helps to promote smoothness.

Cubic interpolation ensures smooth trajectories as gradients are matched at each point. You can play with seeing this cubic interpolation in the script trajectories.py.

See mdp/trajectories.py and mdp/commands.py.

MDP

We use the Soft Actor-Critic algorithm for training, with 256 parallel environments in IsaacLab. The policy and Q functions are parameterised as MLPs with hidden dimensions $[256, 256]$.

The control policy runs at 30Hz, with a simulator decimation of 2 (ie physics timesteps are 1/60s).

Observations

  • Proprioception (joint position, joint velocities)
    • history length: 5
  • Action history
    • history length: 5
  • Future trajectory points
    • future length: 10 (ie the robot sees the 10 next target positions and velocities)

Actions

  • Relative joint actions, with EMA smoothing. The update rule is: 
    targets = prev_targets + moving_average * actions
    • moving_average: 0.2

Using relative actions with smoothing showed drastically smoother and less jerky actions compared to predicting absolute joint angles.

Rewards

  • Position error
    • −0.1 · ‖p − p*‖
  • Fine grained position reward (saturates near the target)
    • +0.1 · (1 − tanh(‖p − p*‖ / 0.1))
  • Velocity error (task the robot with tracking rather than just reaching)
    • −0.05 · ‖v − v*‖
  • Fine grained velocity reward
    • +0.1 · (1 − tanh(‖v − v*‖ / 0.2))
  • Joint velocity penalty
    • −0.007 · ‖q̇‖₁

The robot is rewarded for matching the target end-effector velocity and position. Matching velocity as well as position helps to produce smoother trajectories.

The joint velocity penalty is to encourage the robot to avoid pointless actions which don't contribute to trajectory tracking (eg rotating the hand), since the robot has 7 DoFs and so its joint angles are under-defined.

Evaluation

A set of pre-defined trajectories is used for training. These comprise circles, figures-of-eight and trajectories between randomly sampled points.

In line with test-train splits in supervised learning, the evaluation is then done on unseen test trajectories. We test whether the policy is general enough to perform on these unseen tasks.

To judge performance, for each time step we calculate the Euclidean distance between the target position and the achieved position. We then report the root mean square of the error at each timestep, and the integral over the entire trajectory.

Robustness

We explore the approach's robustness under several sources of uncertainty.

Action noise

We add Gaussian noise to the policy's actions, which is unobservable to the policy. The effect of this can be seen below

Action delay

We delay the policy's actions by an integer number of timesteps to simulate control latency. We ablate whether action history helps the policy control for this.

Generalisation

We also explore how many train-time trajectories are needed before the policy can generalise to unseen test-time trajectories.

Out of bounds

Targets are sampled within the Franka's reachable volume, so waypoints are always reachable.
