Introduction

The RL trained policy is able to control the Franka robot to follow arbitrary trajectories supplied at test time, in the format of a list of $(x_i, y_i, z_i, t_i)$ points. For example:

Averaged over the five held-out trajectories, the policy (trained without action noise) tracks the target end-effector path to $8.0\,\text{mm}$ RMSE with a mean jitter of $0.20\,\text{m/s}^2$.

In the rest of this page, I describe trajectories with interactive 3D plots rather than rendered videos.

Approach

This section describes the design decisions, including trajectory and MDP design.

Trajectory

  • A trajectory is defined as a sequence of $(x_i, y_i, z_i, t_i)$ points.
  • I always use periodic trajectories.
  • The robot does cubic spline interpolation between the provided waypoints. This outputs a target position at each RL step, matching the robot's control frequency.
  • The analytic gradient of this spline is also calculated, and used as the target velocity.

RL

  • I use my custom SAC implementation, with 256 environments.
  • The policy and Q functions are parameterised as MLPs with hidden dimensions $[256, 256]$.

MDP

The control policy runs at 30Hz, with a simulator decimation of 2.

Observations

Observation history length: 5

  • Proprioception (joint position, joint velocities)
  • Action history
  • Target trajectory
    • The robot observes the 10 next target positions and velocities, according to the spline.
    • This observation is excluded from the history length.

Actions

  • Relative joint actions, with EMA smoothing. The update rule is: $$\text{targets} = \text{prev\_targets} + \text{moving\_average} \cdot \text{actions}$$
    • Moving average: 0.2

Using relative actions with smoothing showed drastically smoother and less jerky actions compared to predicting absolute joint angles (see Are relative actions better?).

Rewards

  • Position error
    • $-0.1 \cdot \lVert p - p^* \rVert$
  • Fine grained position reward
    • $+0.1 \cdot (1 - \tanh(\lVert p - p^* \rVert / 0.1))$
  • Velocity error
    • $-0.05 \cdot \lVert v - v^* \rVert$
  • Fine grained velocity reward
    • $+0.1 \cdot (1 - \tanh(\lVert v - v^* \rVert / 0.2))$
  • Joint velocity penalty
    • $-0.007 \cdot \lVert \dot{q} \rVert_1$

The robot is rewarded for matching the target end-effector velocity and position. Matching velocity as well as position helps to produce smoother trajectories.

The joint velocity penalty is to encourage the robot to avoid pointless actions which don't contribute to trajectory tracking (eg. rotating the hand), since the robot has 7 DoFs and so its joint angles are under-defined.

Evaluation

A set of pre-defined trajectories is used for training. These comprise circles, figures-of-eight and trajectories between randomly sampled points.

In line with test-train splits in supervised learning, the evaluation is then done on unseen test trajectories. I test whether the policy is general enough to perform on these unseen tasks.

To judge tracking performance, for each time step I calculate the Euclidean distance between the target position and the achieved position. I then report its root mean square over timesteps.

To evaluate the jitter of the policies, I report the average absolute acceleration over timesteps.

How many train trajectories are needed to generalise?

  • I also explore limiting the number of trajectories seen during training, and see what effect this has on tracking unseen test time trajectories.
  • Training on a single trajectory overfits badly, but test-error reduces sharply once the policy sees >3 train trajectories.

Robustness

I explore the approach's robustness under several sources of uncertainty.

Action noise

  • I explore adding Gaussian noise to the actions.
  • This noise is unobservable to the policy.
  • I ablate the result of adding this noise at train and test time independently.
  • You can toggle how much noise was added during each stage in the interactive figure below.
  • Unsurprisingly, test time noise makes trajectories deviate further from their plan.
  • Training with noise makes the policy better able to adapt to noise at test time, but harms performance when the noise is removed at test time.

Action delay

  • I delay the policy's actions to simulate control latency.
  • I ablate whether feeding the policy its action history for several timesteps lets it adapt to this delay.
  • Without an action delay, the action history has a small effect on the trajectory accuracy.
  • However, with delayed actions, action history is critical. Without it, the policy cannot know the actions already outputted, and so we see very high error.

Out of bounds

Targets are sampled within the Franka's reachable volume, so waypoints are always reachable.
